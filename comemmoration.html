Commemoration Period

Each year Rwanda sets apart the 100 days from April 7 to June 30 to commemorate the 1994 Genocide against the Tutsi. This national time of remembrance is known as the “Kwibuka” period. As a nation, Rwandans take time to reflect on this dark time in our history and to honor victims of the 1994 Genocide against the Tutsi, survivors, and the heroes who saved lives. During this time, those whose lives were cut short are remembered with love and dignity. The three words often used during commemoration period, “remember, unite, and renew,” help people around the globe to reflect on the past while reminding that our actions today will influence the lives of future generations.

Remember to Heal

During the commemoration period, Rwandans no longer see ourselves as Hutu and Tutsi. We are Rwandans, we speak the same language, we are brothers and sisters. Genocide has severely affected us and changed our lives forever, but we decided as a nation to heal. We hope to build a promising future for our children where hatred does not have voice. We work to remind ourselves and the world that genocide is stoppable and preventable. We use this time to tell the world that peace is possible.

Remember to Reflect

The commemoration period also offers a designated time for our nation and the world to examine the deliberate actions that lead to the events in 1994 and to declare “Never Again Genocide.” It is important that world leaders seriously consider their responsibility to render justice to victims, to survivors, and to Rwanda. During this time, we reiterate the importance that we, as members of a global community, must fully commit to root out genocide ideology and fight for a world free from any form of discrimination. We remember the victims of the 1994 Genocide against the Tutsi, honor their lives, and remind the survivors that their lives have purpose.

Remember to Honor

Even today, bodies of victims of the 1994 Genocide against the Tutsi are being found in mass graves—over 20 years later. During the "Kwibuka" period, survivors work together to find bodies of loved ones so that they can be buried with dignity in memorial sites where they will be honored and remembered for eternity. The commemoration period is a difficult time for survivors. It brings back memories the loved ones we lost and the destruction of all we called our own. It reminds us of our loneliness. Still, survivors do not seek revenge, nor are they bitter because of this emphasis on remembering. Instead, the commemoration period serves to restore the dignity of our people. We take steps to build a prosperous future where each one of us can sustain ourselves, feel safe and valued. Survivors assure each other that our lives have purpose and can impact the world in a beautiful way.

Help Survivors

The search for and proper burial of victims is an emotionally distressing and costly process for survivors. That is why we need the support of our friends from all over the world who understand that survivors are critical in the process of a lasting peace on our planet.

To help us remember our beloved with dignity, and support efforts for justice and peace, please click on donate or visit the Get Involved page. Your compassion and love for the most vulnerable among us, especially during the difficult commemoration period, is highly valued.
